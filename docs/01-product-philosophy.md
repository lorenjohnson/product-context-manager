# Product Philosophy

## Working premise

The work here is not trying to replace judgment with a product framework.

It is trying to support a mode of **product stewardship** that is authored, constrained, humane, and interpretable by others. The point is not to become less subjective, but to become more responsible with subjectivity: to shape software from within a situated point of view, while remaining open to signals from others and disciplined by constraints.

This approach rejects two equal and opposite failures:

1. flattening people into market segments, personas, or generalized demographic abstractions
2. retreating into pure authorship with no method for scope, exclusion, or revision

The center is a product practice that treats software as a designed relation: between a maker, a user, a world, and a set of pressures that include but are not limited to utility, commerce, time, and survival.

## Core orientation

The products imagined here are not purely utilitarian tools, but neither are they indifferent to usefulness.

They are meant to make room for a more playful, horizontal, exploratory, and alive relation to life and work, while still feeding back into lived usefulness at particular points of contact. The product does not need to justify itself only through narrow productivity. But it should be able to show why it matters in the world as it is.

That means the design challenge is not:

> How do we optimize users toward a task?

It is closer to:

> How do we create a form through which people can become more alive, less narrowed, and more capable within the bad world that presently exists?

## Working principles

### 1. Constraint before expansion

A product should be shaped first by the real limits of time, attention, appetite, and stewardship.

Constraint is not a regrettable reduction of the work. It is part of the form of the work. A feature or direction that cannot be genuinely cared for, maintained, and evolved should be treated skeptically no matter how interesting it is.

### 2. Meaning before market abstraction

A product should be understood through the kinds of experiences, openings, and shifts it makes possible, not primarily through demographic segmentation or generic growth language.

The key question is not only who the user is. It is also: in what situation do they reach for this, and what kind of movement are they hoping for?

### 3. Playfulness with consequence

Play, drift, surprise, and exploratory relation are not escapes from seriousness. They are often the conditions through which new seriousness becomes possible.

The product can be playful and still matter. In fact, play may be part of how it proves its relevance.

### 4. Productive contact without capitulation

Software can acknowledge the pressures of capitalist life, work, productivity, and survival without being fully defined by those pressures.

The aim is not to build a fantasy outside the world, nor a tool fully surrendered to it. The aim is to make something that helps people become more resourced, imaginative, and less afraid within it.

### 5. Interpretation is part of the craft

Users may request literal features. The designer’s task is not merely to obey the request, but to interpret the need, desire, friction, or possibility under it.

That interpretive act is not a flaw to eliminate. It is part of design responsibility.

### 6. Removal is legitimate

A product is not only built by addition. It is also built by refusal, deletion, reframing, and starting over.

Functionality that weakens the product’s relation to its own center can be removed even after it has existed, so long as that decision is made with care toward actual use and trust.

## Top references

These references are not being treated as authorities to obey. They are anchor points that help articulate a practice.

### Shape Up — Basecamp / 37signals

**Reference:** *Shape Up*, especially the distinction between **appetite** and estimation, and the general practice of fixed-time / variable-scope shaping.  
https://basecamp.com/shapeup

This is central because it restores dignity to constraint and judgment. Rather than pretending scope should expand until every demand is satisfied, it begins from what kind of time, energy, and commitment is actually available and worthwhile. It also leaves room for taste and authorship: the team is not just collecting requests, but interpreting what kind of response is worth shaping. For this product philosophy, Shape Up is less valuable as startup process than as a design ethic grounded in form, stewardship, and appetite.

### Tools for Conviviality — Ivan Illich

**Reference:** Ivan Illich, *Tools for Conviviality*  
https://books.google.com/books/about/Tools_for_Conviviality.html?id=jbh_PwAACAAJ

This is central because it asks whether tools enlarge human autonomy and creative agency, or whether they subordinate people to systems. That question is much closer to the spirit of this work than a standard product-growth frame. A convivial tool is not merely efficient; it supports meaningful, situated action without turning the user into an appendage of the system. This makes Illich especially useful as a philosophical counterweight when building software that should be enabling, playful, and liberatory rather than merely sticky, optimizing, or extractive.

### A Pattern Language — Christopher Alexander et al.

**Reference:** Christopher Alexander et al., *A Pattern Language*  
https://christopher-alexander-ces-archive.org/book/a-pattern-language/

This is central because it suggests a way of working with design knowledge that is neither purely abstract nor purely prescriptive. Instead of moving from rigid doctrine to implementation, it gathers recurring patterns that can be adapted locally and recombined with care. For software and product thinking, this is useful because it offers a way to document and transmit design intelligence without collapsing everything into top-down methodology. It supports structure without deadening variation.

### Humane by Design — Jon Yablonski

**Reference:** Humane by Design  
https://humanebydesign.com/

This is central because it provides a practical check against the attention-extractive defaults of digital product design. Its emphasis on principles such as respectful, intentional, transparent, and finite helps translate broad philosophical commitments into interface-level judgment. It is less foundational than Illich or Shape Up here, but very useful as a bridge between humane values and concrete product / UX decisions.

## Relation to more conventional product methods

The references above are being favored not because classic product management is useless, but because it often begins at the wrong level for this kind of work.

Conventional PM often starts with personas, roadmap logic, prioritization frameworks, feature sets, and business outcomes. Those can be useful later. But they tend to under-describe:

- the role of makerly judgment
- the positive function of constraints
- the dignity of playful or non-instrumental experience
- the political and ethical stakes of tools
- the need to treat removal and refusal as first-class product moves

The aim here is to begin from a better center, then borrow selectively from more conventional methods where useful.
