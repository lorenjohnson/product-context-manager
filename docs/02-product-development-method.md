# Product Development Method

## Purpose

This method is meant to be usable by a solo builder or a small team working in an LLM-assisted environment.

It is deliberately compact. It should be legible enough to use repeatedly, but light enough to preserve intuition, experimentation, and authorship.

## Concrete constraints

The most immediate constraints named so far are:

- **time** — there is limited time available to develop, refine, document, and maintain the work
- **attention** — there is limited cognitive and emotional bandwidth for holding a product and its edge cases in view
- **appetite** — not every interesting direction is equally alive; some paths generate energy and some drain it
- **stewardship capacity** — a feature is not just something to build once, but something to own, explain, revisit, and sometimes remove
- **philosophical coherence** — the product should remain meaningfully related to its own principles rather than accumulating disconnected functionality
- **worldly relevance** — even exploratory or playful products need some convincing point of contact with the conditions of actual life and survival

These are not secondary to the method. They are part of the method.

## A proposed iterative cycle

### 1. Notice the signal

Begin with one of these:

- a recurring internal idea
- a friction in use
- a user request
- a surprising behavior in real use
- a gap between the product’s philosophy and its actual experience

The first task is not to solve it. It is to state it cleanly.

### 2. Interpret the underlying movement

Ask:

- What desire, opening, need, fear, or difficulty seems to live under this signal?
- Is the request literal, or is it pointing toward something deeper?
- Does this belong to the core relation of the product, or is it peripheral?

This step replaces literal feature intake with interpretation.

### 3. Test against principles

Before shaping solutions, ask whether the signal strengthens or weakens the product’s center.

Questions to ask:

- Does this make the product more alive, playful, or humane?
- Does it support a better relation to the world, memory, work, or attention?
- Does it intensify fear, noise, compulsion, or generic utility at the expense of the product’s character?
- Would implementing it bring the product closer to or farther from its own reason for existing?

### 4. Shape within appetite

Decide how much time and energy the problem deserves.

Examples:

- a quick refinement
- a small cycle
- a focused release
- a delayed exploration
- a clear refusal

This is the Shape Up move: define appetite first, then shape the response to fit it.

### 5. Prototype or implement the minimum meaningful response

Build the smallest version that can honestly test the direction.

Not the smallest possible ticket. The smallest **meaningful** form.

That may be:

- a UI adjustment
- a new interaction mode
- a framing / onboarding change
- a documentation change
- a hidden prototype
- a temporary branch
- the decision not to build, but to clarify language instead

### 6. Release with explicit intent

Each release should be understandable in terms of what it is trying to improve.

A useful release note format is:

- what changed
- what motivated the change
- what kind of experience or relation it is trying to improve
- what remains uncertain

### 7. Evaluate in use

Evaluation should not collapse to metrics alone.

Look at:

- actual repeated use
- felt quality of the experience
- friction or confusion
- unintended narrowing or over-complexity
- whether the feature deepens trust
- whether the feature proves relevant in real life without flattening the product into pure utility

### 8. Decide: keep, deepen, revise, remove, or restart

Every cycle should end with a real decision.

Possible outcomes:

- keep as is
- deepen and refine
- simplify
- reframe in documentation
- remove entirely
- start over from the underlying desire rather than the current implementation

## On deletion, rollback, and starting over

Any healthy product system must permit subtraction.

That includes:

- removing features that dilute the product
- changing direction after real use reveals a mismatch
- simplifying interfaces that became over-expressive
- rolling back experiments that reduce trust or clarity
- restarting an area from first principles rather than defending sunk cost

This has to be balanced against user trust and actual engagement. A product cannot thrash endlessly. But preserving every decision for fear of change is just another kind of drift.

The tension is real and important:

- too much volatility weakens trust
- too much preservation weakens integrity

That tension should be watched consciously rather than solved once and for all.

## Suggested decision filter

Before adding or keeping a feature, ask:

1. What signal gave rise to this?
2. What underlying movement do I think it points to?
3. Why is this response better than the most literal one?
4. What appetite am I willing to spend here?
5. Does this increase or decrease the product’s coherence?
6. What would make me later remove this with a clear conscience?

## Practical note for LLM-assisted development

In an AI-assisted workflow, this method becomes even more useful because implementation can outrun thought.

So the guardrails should live in docs and be revisited often. The model can help generate options, but it should not determine:

- the product’s center
- the legitimacy of a constraint
- the difference between novelty and coherence
- whether a feature deserves stewardship

Those remain human product judgments.

## What success looks like

A successful cycle does not necessarily produce more functionality.

It may produce:

- a sharper center
- a cleaner refusal
- a smaller and better feature
- better wording
- a more intelligible onboarding path
- a restored trust in the product’s direction

The method is working when the product becomes more itself, not merely more full.
